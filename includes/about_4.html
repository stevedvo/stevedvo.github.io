Formula 1

I LOVE music! There is not a time in the day that I don't have some music playing somewhere - unless there is some other audio to tune into instead e.g. television, cinema. Be it at home on the computer, doing housework, in the car, at work, and even to help me sleep, there is always something playing somewhere to fill the silence!

Which is quite a turnaround, really, considering how uninterested I was in music as a child and in my early teens. In my younger years, the music I was typically exposed to at home was either classical music [which I rarely listen to these days but I do actually quite like] or whatever was in the mainstream charts. This being the 1980's, this was really not a superb decade for mainstream chart music [in my opinion]! The only music I really enjoyed was Status Quo - I had a copy of the 25th Anniversary compilation album on cassette that I played over and over and over...

It wasn't really until the mid-1990's that the chart-topping music actually started to grab my interest. I didn't know it then, but this was the explosion of indie/alternative/Britpop. What I call "proper" bands were flooding the charts: Oasis & Blur being particularly popular. Guitars & drums - none of this 80's synth-pop rubbish!

And so it began. I started listening to the Radio 1 Breakfast Show with Chris Evans, and watching TFI Friday, and over the course of a few years the floodgates were opened and I was into dozens of bands, including especially Shed Seven, The Charlatans, The Stone Roses, Reef, Pulp, Suede, Ocean Colour Scene, to name but a few. The first band I saw live was Shed Seven when they played at Sheffield University (where I was studying at the time) and they just blew me away. They were (and still are!) one of the best live acts out there. My love of music and attending gigs was complete.


DRUMS

Throughout my school years I dabbled a little with trying to play instruments: the recorder [of course!], keyboard, guitar - but I was never really any good. At that time I didn't listen to or understand music enough, or really have the inclination to improve my skills. But in my 20's when my interest in music was exponentially higher, I began to enjoy listening more carefully to the individual elements that made up each song: the structure of the verses, chorus, bridge; and isolating each of the instruments being played: lead/bass/rhythm guitars and the drums. I found that of all of the elements it was the drums and the beat that I particularly liked, and realised I could quite easily tap along with my hands & feet at different rates to match the full/half/quarter beat - and of all the instruments in your typical rock band the drums was the one that I'd never really had a proper go at but became convinced that this was something that I would enjoy and would be reasonably good at!

It wasn't until I was around 30 that I became more serious about trying to learn how to play. I had a couple of issues to overcome: having the space to put a drum kit in the house, and not being antisocial and bothering the neighbours with all the racket. My solution was to completely board out my loft space and put an electronic drum kit up there. It took some doing, it was quite the largest DIY project I'd attempted up to that point, but the end goal of being able to set up a drum kit and have some serious fun kept me going! I borrowed a tutorial book from a friend who was a couple of years ahead of me in learning to play, bought myself a Yamaha electronic kit and away I went.

I had a few weeks learning how to play on my own and becoming familiar with how to put together a basic rock beat, and then had a few proper lessons from a local drummer. It was encouraging to know that my timing was already pretty good for a beginner. The lessons were always enjoyable but always difficult to try and get my head around new things - or not so much my head but just trying to get my limbs to do the right things at the right time! Having my own kit available was fab and helped enormously to be able to practice, practice, practice before the next lesson. I'd go into the lesson feeling quite good and confident in my abilities but then feel like a complete amateur again when learning something new!

Only really had a few formal lessons, but from then on once I'd mastered the basics I found it was just a case of listening very carefully to the percussion on every song and imagining how I would need to move my arms & feet to hit the right drums & cymbals to replicate what I could hear.

I was fortunate to live only a short walk away from a pub which has live bands on every Saturday and Bank Holiday weekend. If I had nothing else on with friends/family, this was my default option for a Saturday night: quite happy to go there on my own, have a few pints of Guinness and stand/sit as close to the band as possible to watch the drummer like a hawk doing his/her thing. Listening to and watching the drums being played, and practicing at home whenever possible - this had become a great passion of mine. For fun, and to unwind after a hard day at the office, there's not much better than having a good old crack on the kit.

The electronic kit is great for learning new things and improving the muscle memory, but it will never match the range of sounds availiable and the exqusite noise that comes with a proper acoustic kit... which was what I particularly missed from the formal lessons. So it wasn't long before I bought my own acoustic kit and set that up in my loft too, and over time added to the basic setup with additional crash & ride cymbals. MUCH better - although I was always careful to play this noisy kit only when the neighbour wasn't home.
